Choosing a Stats Test

Inferential Statistics

  • Studies are conducted to test a hypothesis and to derive inferences from sample results about the population.
  • The goal is to assess differences between groups (comparison), establish an association between two variables (correlation), predict one variable (regression), or look for agreement between measures (agreement).

Paired vs unpaired

  • Observations made on the same individual (before and after) are usually matched or paired.
    • Data are considered paired if the values in one set are likely to be influenced by those in the other.
  • Comparisons made between individuals are unpaired or unmatched.

Types of Data

  • The test chosen will depend on the type of data: categorical (normal or ordinal) or numerical (skewed or normally distributed).
  • Tests used to analyse normally distributed data are known as parametric tests.
  • and have a nonparametric counterpart used for distribution-free data.
  • Parametric Tests:
    • Assume that the sample data is normally distributed.
    • Normality Testing can confirm this assumption.
    • And that it has the same characteristics as the population.
    • more powerful and better able to detect differences between groups.
  • Nonparametric tests:
    • Make no normality assumption.
    • less efficient at identifying significant differences.
  • Time-to-event data require a specialised analysis, known as survival analysis.

How many measurements?

  • The test also depends on whether two or more than two measurements are being compared.
  • This includes more than two groups (unmatched data) or more than two measurements in a group (matched data).

Normality Testing

  • We can test normality either analytically or graphically.
  • The most common normality tests are:
    • Kolmogorov-Smirnov Test
    • Shapiro-Wilk Test
    • Anderson-Darling Test
  • Where H0H_0H0​ is that the data is normally distributed.
  • The test will give a ppp-value which will allow us to reject or not reject the null hypothesis.

Issues

  • The ppp-value depends on the sample size.
  • So for small sample sizes, the ppp-value may be much larger than 0.050.050.05, but for large samples it could be a lot smaller.
  • To avoid this people tend to use graphical methods.

Graphical Tests

  • We can test graphically using Q-Q plots or histograms.
  • A Quantile-Quantile plot (Q-Q) compares theoretical quantiles that the data should have if they were perfectly normal.
  • If the data were perfectly normally distributed, all points would lie on the line.
  • The further the data deviate from the line, the less normally distributed they are.
